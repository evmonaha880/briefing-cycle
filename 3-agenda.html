Phase 3 · Agenda design

The customer's problems set the agenda.

Read the session titles alone: they should describe Target's situation, never a product catalog. One intent statement aligns the briefing team; every logistical decision is made before the day so the room gets full presence.

The intent, in one sentence

Every session moves one named Target problem toward one committed next step. Any block that doesn't serve that gets cut.

This is the sentence every session leader plans against. It survives contact with the day when the run-of-show doesn't.

Two design rules did the real work here. First: session 4 exists because discovery said in-stocks is their stated pain. That forecasting demos well had no vote. Second: the change-management session is framed bottom-up. The recurring executive question in any briefing is "how do I get my people to change how they're working?" The honest answer is adoption: teams pick tools up because the tools help, and no mandate produces that. Hidden AI use is already widespread inside large enterprises, and the levers that surface it are credible commitments and visible incentives.24 Target walks in with an unusual advantage: fourteen months of organizational simplification mean the change program has already run ahead of the AI program.11 One provocation is reserved for session 5, and it's the day's sharpest question: is this an operating-model commitment, or a portfolio of pilots?24

Under the hood — how this phase was actually produced

Claude generated three candidate agenda architectures from the discovery synthesis (problem-ordered, capability-ordered, and stakeholder-ordered) with per-session from→to lines; the human picked problem-ordered, cut two blocks that failed the intent sentence, and wrote the immersion elements. Leader prep briefs are generated per session from the same synthesis; in a live cycle each leader gets theirs the same hour the agenda locks. Time on this phase: see the ledger.
