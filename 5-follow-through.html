Phase 5 · Follow-through

Most of the value is created after the room.

Most programs treat what happens after the room as courtesy. This cycle treats it as the deliverable: a close-out package inside a week, a ladder that books the next step before momentum cools, a sponsor cadence with dates, and a record anyone can query. Success is what the customer does next, not what they said on the way out.

Days 1–5: the close-out package

InstrumentMechanicClock
Thank-yous, same dayEvery attendee and every internal contributor gets a specific, human thank-you before anything else goes out. Small, and the fastest trust signal the program sends.T+0–1
Dual feedbackTwo surveys: the customer scores the experience and their intent; the account team scores sales utility. Divergence between the two is itself a diagnostic: a briefing the customer loved and the account team found useless is a failure with good ratings.T+1–3
Internal debrief callAccount team + briefing team, against a written checklist: what was committed, what surprised, what the customer's questions revealed about their stage. Always a live call.T+2
Post-briefing action planThe commitments table from the room, expanded: every row an owner, a date, and a definition of done, written in customer-benefit language.T+5
Sponsor introductionA named executive sponsor attached to the account, introduced in the action-plan note, with the first check-in already dated.T+5

What Pilot 1 is playing for (illustrative sizing)

Two facts anchor it: Target's FY2025 net sales were $104.8B,1 and its COO named in-stocks and findability "the biggest friction points for our guests," especially in food.3 The rest is arithmetic on exposed assumptions: if food is roughly a fifth of sales (an assumption; the 10-K category table replaces it before anyone quotes this), and a missed shelf converts to a lost sale somewhere between 30% and 60% of the time, then one point of in-stock improvement in food is worth roughly $60M–$130M a year. Every uncited input above is labeled, and the pilot charter's first job is replacing each assumption with the customer's own number by Day 30. The same discipline sizes Pilot 2: roughly half of what Target sells is sourced internationally, and Target is importer of record for most of its owned brands,7 so detention, demurrage, and empty-repositioning line items give that agent a measurable prize of its own.

Measurement: an instrumented scoreboard of influenced pipeline

The number this program answers for is program-influenced pipeline and expansion, the finance-grade frame practitioners at mature programs describe: measuring briefing effectiveness "on deal acceleration, and influencing open pipeline" ⚠️ single-source, a ServiceNow finance leader's own description.20 The instrumentation is structural: link the opportunity at intake, review influence quarterly, survey twice (immediately, and delayed at 90 days when intent has become behavior).

The instrument is one required intake field. At booking, the account executive names the opportunity this briefing is meant to influence and the dollar figure they would attribute to it. At Day 90 the same row is reconciled against the CRM: what was attributed, what actually moved, and the gap, with reasons.

Pilot 1 expansion opportunity, named by the AE at booking$1.2M attributed$800K advanced to committed forecast; gap noted (scope cut to two categories)

The honesty rule, applied to our own industry's favorite numbers: ServiceNow's briefing program is often credited with $855M in influenced revenue, a figure that traces to one employee's LinkedIn profile and no published source. Industry survey stats (71% of attendees deciding to purchase, deal values up an average 31%) circulate through vendor whitepapers with inconsistent numbers between relays.21 We cite them as what they are, folklore with a direction but no audit trail, and instrument this program so its number is defensible: attribution recorded at intake, reviewed against the CRM, reported with its assumptions attached. A scoreboard you could bet on.

This is true if: opportunities are linked at intake with discipline (the single failure point of every attribution system), and the delayed survey actually runs. Both are written into the action plan as owned steps with names attached.

The last beat: the record

Within a day of the briefing (about thirty minutes of work), everything the cycle produced is centralized into one structured, queryable place: the request, the qualification decision, the discovery synthesis with its confidence levels, the agenda and its from→to lines, the document that traveled, every commitment with its owner and date, both surveys, and the 30/60/90 as it unfolds. Structured so that a coworker, or an AI agent, can find what they need without asking anyone. Anthropic's own briefing lead has described exactly this practice after his briefings: "Maybe thirty minutes each time."22

The lineage here is longer than it looks: Salesforce ran its briefing program on shared, reusable documents as far back as 2019 ⚠️ single-source, Salesforce's own post23. The queryable record completes what that started, with one difference none of the predecessors had: a search layer that answers a question by surfacing the exact line it came from.

You have already seen this cycle's record. It is the page you entered through: the front door of this site is the post-engagement record of the cycle you just walked, holding every artifact, every commitment, and the ledger of what each phase cost. The survey score closes the measurement loop. The cycle ends with its own memory, organized for the next person who needs it.

Three moments the record is built for (illustrative)

Day 90, the go/no-go. The pilot review opens with the charter the room agreed to: the baseline, the owner, the graduation criteria written at Day 30. Everyone argues about the results, because what was promised is already on the page. Anthropic's own published enterprise guidance names this exact discipline, graduation criteria that keep pilots from getting stuck in pilot phase; the record is where they live and get enforced.18

Day 45, the quiet slip. The master-data audit committed at Day 30 hasn't moved. Because every commitment carries an owner and a date, the slip surfaces on a status pull rather than dying in an inbox. It matters more here than most places: data quality is the pilot's named prerequisite, and this cycle's own discovery form holds the record of what bad master data did to this company at its Canadian launch ⚠️ single-source, Castaldo.10

Six months out, a different retailer. A colleague preparing a briefing for another grocery chain pulls this cycle's skeleton in an afternoon: the intent sentence, the charter format, the flag convention. Each customer's content stays its own. The structure is what compounds.

Under the hood: how this phase was actually produced

No public source describes how Anthropic's own briefing program runs its follow-through: the program is months old. This phase is therefore a proposal, assembled from the strongest published practice across the industry's mature programs (each element cited to its own source), and its instruments (the dual surveys, the debrief checklist, the action-plan format, the 30/60/90) were authored fresh for this cycle: the industry's own versions sit behind association paywalls, which is itself a finding. Claude drafted each instrument from the cited practices; the human decided what a one-person program can actually keep: every instrument here survives contact with a team of one.
